Hypothesis 3 (Diversity Sample)

The use of terms like genetic ancestry as well as national and continental terms have outpaced racial and ethnic terms in biomedical literature.

To test Hypothesis 3, we parsed our dictionary of 2,200+ population terms into a set of more nuanced analytic categories. Our main objective is to test Fujimura and Rajagopalan’s (2011) assertion that researchers now use biogeographic terms more often than race and ethnicity in their studies. Once again, following Panofsky and Bliss (2017), we looked at the use of continent, country, and directional terminology, US-specific terms (corresponding to the US Census and OMB Directive 15 labeling schema), a category of general population terms (i.e. ancestry, race, ethnicity, population, minority, etc.), and a category of all available ethnic terms in our compiled dictionary (see Appendix below).

In support of Fujimura and Rajagopalan (2011), Figures 3A & 3B reveal that continental and country terms outgrew the use of racial, ethnic and US-Specific terminology over the past three decades. More specifically, Figure 3A shows that country terms have exhibited a 35-fold increase followed closely by the use of continental terms. Directional terms, which are both included in the broader continental classification (as they are typically used to refer to specific areas within continents) and broken apart into their own independent line, have also grown notably, but pale in comparison with the rise in continent and country terms. There has also been a noticable uptick in general population terms, which is a bit of catch-all category. We can see that the term population, which is included both in the general population classification in addition to being plotted independently, accounts for more than half of this growth while race and ethnicity makes up considerably less of that category’s ascent. Ethnic terms, in contrast, have only increased marginally, growing at roughly the same rate as US-specific terminology since 1990.

Figure 3B displays the proportional changes in the use of population terminology. Again, country terms have grown the most; surging from 12% of abstracts to now being mentioned in 35% of abstracts. Continental terms have also risen by about 15% over that time period. General population terms, the term population, and directional terms follow analogous patterns, rising 10%, 8% and 6% respectively since 1990. In contrast, there has been almost no variation in the use of racial, ethnic or US-specific terminology over the past three decades. While ethnic terms has stayed consistently low across our analysis period, race/ethnicity and US-specific terminology followed a parabolic curve that increases up until the mid-2000s before declining back to its baseline in 2017.

Main Takeaway

Together, these analyses reveal that national and continental terms outgrew the use of racial, ethnic and US-Specific - a trend that holds for both the raw counts and proportions in Figures 3A & 3B. These findings strongly support Fujimura and Rajagopalan’s (2011) thesis that national and continental terms are now more commonly used than racial and ethnic terms in the scientific literature, suggesting that scholars of diversity must be attentive to the emerging strategies that biomedical researchers use to enact population differences in their research. Focusing solely on discourses related to race and ethnicity likely fails to capture the more subtle ways that biomedical scholars implemented new and potentially dangerous forms of population difference in their research.
